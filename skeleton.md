# Session Skeleton — News in the Age of Recommendation: How Platforms Like TikTok Reshape Who Sees What

## Session Metadata

- **Slot:** Day 1, 11:30–13:00 (90 minutes)
- **Audience:** Mid-career practicing journalists, mostly from across Europe, some from Africa and other regions; highly engaged, value active participation
- **Day-1 theme:** Transformation of the information environment, shifting dynamics of news authority, implications for journalism
- **Format:** Discussion-led. Every block opens with open questions to the room; the input then organizes and reframes what participants said
- **Language:** English

## Through-line

The shift from subscription-based distribution to recommendation systems has quietly transferred editorial power from newsrooms to platforms. This session uses TikTok as the clearest case to show what that transfer means in practice: who actually sees political news, what producers must do to be seen, what this does to the quality of public discourse, and why data transparency is now a professional issue for journalism, not just a regulatory one. The session moves from the participants' own experience (opening) to platform mechanics (blocks 1–3) to consequences and responses (block 4, closing).

## Takeaway map

| # | Takeaway | Block |
|---|----------|-------|
| 1 | Subscription → recsys transition creates a huge power asymmetry between platform and producers/users | Block 1 |
| 2 | Two attention streams: niche political consumers vs. everyday users exposed around focus events | Block 2 |
| 3 | The everyday stream centers on moderate/centrist news; extreme content gets less exposure | Block 2 |
| 4 | Generalized competition for attention: incentives favor format and attention-bait over news quality | Block 3 |
| 5 | Virality is a dynamic equilibrium with the platform: no magic recipe; following platform-pushed trends means accepting its agenda-setting | Block 3 |

## Opening — Where does news authority live now? (11:30–11:40, 10 min)

**Key message:** Set the frame for the day: the question is no longer only "what do we publish?" but "who decides what gets seen?"

**Open questions (verbatim, ready to use):**

- "Think of the last story you published that really travelled. Where did your audience actually encounter it — your front page, your app, a search result, or someone's feed?"
- "In your newsroom today, who has more influence over what your audience reads: your editor-in-chief, or an algorithm you've never met?"

**Input talking points:**

- Harvest answers on a flipchart in two columns: "channels we control" vs. "channels we don't." The second column will dominate; let that visual do the arguing.
- Name the day-1 theme: news authority is migrating from institutions to infrastructures. This session looks inside one such infrastructure (TikTok) to understand the new rules.
- One sentence of positioning: this draws on large-scale computational research on TikTok (collection covering roughly 80% of global TikTok production), not on platform PR or anecdote.

**Facilitation note:** Keep this brisk and warm; the goal is to surface that nearly everyone already lives the problem. Pick 2–3 answers you can call back to later ("remember what X said about reach collapsing overnight").

## Block 1 — From subscription to recommendation: the great power shift (11:40–11:55, 15 min)

**Key message (Takeaway 1):** The transition from subscription logic to recommendation logic creates a structural power asymmetry: the platform, not the producer, owns the relationship with the audience.

**Open questions:**

- "What did it mean to 'have an audience' twenty years ago — and what does it mean now?"
- "Has your outlet ever seen its reach collapse without explanation? What did you do — and whom could you even ask?"

**Input talking points:**

- Subscription logic: the audience is the producer's asset. Readers chose a masthead; distribution was predictable; the relationship was direct (print run, RSS, newsletter, follower list that actually got served).
- Recommendation logic: the audience is the platform's asset. The feed is assembled per user, per session; following an account no longer guarantees seeing its content.
- The algorithm as editor-in-chief (reuse framing from the ahuntsic-2026 deck): it performs the two classic media functions — informational (what content) and attentional (whose content, which topics) — but without a masthead, without accountability, and without a phone number to call.
- Asymmetry in both directions: producers cannot see why they are shown or hidden; users cannot see why their feed looks the way it does. Both depend on a counterpart that observes them completely and explains nothing.

**Facilitation note:** The reach-collapse question reliably produces strong stories; let one or two run, then label them as the asymmetry in action rather than individual bad luck. Resist solving anything here; blocks 3 and 4 do that.

## Block 2 — Who actually sees political news? The two attention streams (11:55–12:10, 15 min)

**Key message (Takeaways 2 + 3):** Political attention on recommendation platforms flows in two distinct streams, and the bigger one is more moderate than journalists tend to assume.

**Open questions (run as a prediction exercise):**

- "Give me a number: what share of content on TikTok would you guess is political?"
- "And who consumes it — picture the typical viewer of political TikTok. Describe them."
- "Do you expect the algorithm to push people toward more extreme content over time? Show of hands."

**Input talking points:**

- Reveal against their predictions (evidence from the US political content study in the ahuntsic-2026 deck):
  - Stream 1 — niche users: a small set of users who consume political content heavily and constantly. They sustain the everyday political conversation on the platform.
  - Stream 2 — everyday users: the vast majority, who encounter political news only around focus events (elections, scandals, crises, wars). Attention spikes track events, then recedes.
- The counterintuitive finding: the everyday stream concentrates on moderate, centrist-leaning news. Extreme content gets comparatively less exposure on the platform than the "radicalization machine" narrative suggests; centrist content dominates engagement.
- Implication for journalists: the casual majority is reachable, but only in event windows, and the content that wins those windows is broadly palatable, not extreme. The audience you imagine when you fear TikTok is mostly the small niche stream.

**Facilitation note:** The show-of-hands on radicalization is the pivot of the session; most hands will go up, and the correction lands hard. Be precise about scope — this is about exposure distribution on the platform, not a claim that radical content is harmless or absent. Expect pushback from participants with strong priors; welcome it and park the "but how would we verify this ourselves?" question explicitly for Block 4.

## Block 3 — The attention market: producing news under platform rules (12:10–12:30, 20 min)

**Key message (Takeaways 4, 5, 7):** On a recommendation platform every producer competes with every other producer for the same attention; the winning strategies are dictated by the platform's trend dynamics; and the promise that anyone can break through is statistically an illusion.

**Open questions:**

- "Who here has a TikTok, Reels, or Shorts strategy in their newsroom? What does 'success' look like there — and who defined that?"
- "Has any of you had something go viral? What did you learn from it — and could you do it again on purpose?"
- "When you make a vertical video version of a story, what survives from the original — and what gets cut?"

**Input talking points:**

- One market, all producers: on the feed, a public broadcaster competes head-to-head with influencers, brands, hobbyists, and propagandists for the same swipe. There is no separate shelf for news. Incentives therefore push toward format and attention-bait — hooks in the first second, emotion over nuance — at the expense of the journalistic qualities that don't show up in watch time (reuse the McLuhan point from the ahuntsic-2026 deck: reformatting content transforms the incentives of production itself; plus the format–algorithm feedback loop: short video makes algorithmic curation viable, algorithmic curation rewards short video).
- Virality is a dynamic equilibrium, not a recipe: whatever "works" decays as everyone copies it; the platform continuously introduces and pushes new trends (sounds, formats, challenges). To stay visible, producers must keep following trends, which means the platform — not the newsroom — sets the meta-agenda of what gets made. Agenda-setting has moved one level up: not which stories, but which forms and rhythms of storytelling.
- The American dream is an illusion: the platform's promise is that anyone can reach millions. In the data, roughly 5% of accounts capture about 93% of attention. Open access to publishing coexists with extreme concentration of visibility — easy entry, brutal inequality of outcome.
- Pull the threads together: journalists who succeed on these platforms are not gaming the system; they are being responsive to it. That responsiveness is exactly the asymmetry from Block 1, now operating on production decisions.

**Facilitation note:** This is the longest block and the one closest to participants' daily work; protect time for at least two viral-experience stories and reframe each as the equilibrium dynamic ("it worked once, the recipe expired"). The "what gets cut in the vertical version" question is the quality-erosion takeaway in their own words — quote their answers back.

## Block 4 — What this does to public discourse, and what journalism can demand (12:30–12:45, 15 min)

**Key message (Takeaways 6 + 8):** The platform-driven acceleration of the news cycle degrades the deliberative quality of public discourse, and the only structural counterweight to the power asymmetry is enforced data transparency with third-party auditing capacity.

**Open questions:**

- "How long does a major story stay alive in your country now, compared to ten years ago? What disappears when a story dies in two days instead of two weeks?"
- "Suppose your editor asks you tomorrow: 'What did TikTok users in our country actually see about the election last week?' Could you report that story? How?"

**Input talking points:**

- Acceleration as a normative problem: deliberation needs time — time for facts to consolidate, for counterarguments to surface, for public opinion to form. Trend-driven attention moves on before that process completes; the next topic occupies the space before the previous one is digested. The result is a public sphere that reacts constantly but deliberates rarely.
- The reporting gap (the concrete case for transparency): every user sees a different feed. Individual experiences — your own account, a colleague's, a screenshot — do not aggregate into a picture of what the platform actually showed people. Journalists today have no legitimate way to answer "what did users experience?" at the population level. The story of the algorithm is currently unreportable from the outside.
- The structural answer: enforced data transparency and third-party auditing capacity for independent researchers — so that claims like the ones presented in Block 2 can be made, checked, and challenged. For the European participants: this is exactly what the DSA's data-access provisions (Article 40) are supposed to deliver; the research behind this session shows both that independent measurement is possible and how hard platforms make it. Pay off the question parked in Block 2: "how would we verify this ourselves?" — this is how.
- Position transparency as a journalistic interest, not just an academic one: auditing capacity for scholars is upstream infrastructure for accountability reporting.

**Facilitation note:** The "could you report that story?" question is the hinge; let the room sit with the honest answer (no) before offering the transparency frame. Participants from outside the EU will note the DSA doesn't cover them — acknowledge it and ask what leverage exists in their context; this seeds the closing discussion.

## Closing discussion — So what for your practice? (12:45–13:00, 15 min)

**Key message:** Translate the structural picture into professional consequences the participants articulate themselves.

**Open questions (pick 2–3 depending on energy):**

- "Given everything we've discussed, what is one thing you would change in how your newsroom approaches platforms — and one thing you would refuse to change?"
- "If TikTok had to answer one question from this room, under oath, what would you ask?"
- "Where does news authority go from here — back to institutions, deeper into platforms, or somewhere we haven't named yet?"

**Input talking points:**

- One-slide recap of the eight takeaways (use the takeaway map table above).
- Close the loop with the opening flipchart: the "channels we don't control" column is not a reason for fatalism; Blocks 2–4 showed that the dynamics are knowable, measurable, and therefore contestable.

**Facilitation note:** This block belongs to the room. The "under oath" question reliably produces the best material; if time is short, use only that one. End on the recap slide so the takeaways are the last thing on screen at 13:00.
